# PROLOGUE: The Universe Does Not Care

---

It came from the direction of the sun.

That is the first thing to understand. It came from the direction of the sun 66 million years ago, which means no eye — not the compound eye of a dragonfly, not the lateral line of a fish detecting pressure changes in warm Cretaceous seas — could have seen it coming. The geometry was wrong. The sun was too bright. The sky was too blue. 

The twelve-kilometre rock, which we now call the Chicxulub impactor after the small Mexican town built above its buried scar, entered Earth's atmosphere with enough kinetic energy to equal **ten billion Hiroshima bombs** detonating simultaneously. 

The fireball reached the stratosphere. The shockwave circled the Earth four times. Billions of tonnes of vaporised rock were flung into orbit, re-entering as a global meteor shower that set the sky on fire. There was nowhere to hide. Not in the ocean. Not underground. Not in prayer.

The non-avian dinosaurs — 165 million years of evolutionary success — were functionally extinct within weeks. 76% of all species on Earth followed them into oblivion. 

<div class="gut-punch">
And the universe did not notice.
</div>

---

The universe — 13.8 billion years old, 93 billion light-years across — did not notice. There was no mourning. No record kept. No lesson learned. The asteroid did not regret itself. The void felt nothing. 

<p class="impact-text cyan">The universe does not care whether we live or die.</p>

I want you to feel that. Not as despair, but as *clarity*. Because the moment you truly absorb it — the moment you understand that no divine mechanism guarantees your survival — something extraordinary happens.

<div class="manifesto-box">
YOU BECOME RESPONSIBLE.
</div>

---

### THE AWAKENING

Five million years ago, one branch of mammals developed something unprecedented. Not flight. Not complex eyes. Not social organisation. 

<p class="impact-text purple">They began to wonder.</p>

They began to ask — *why*. We are the descendants of those wondering creatures. In the three hundred thousand years since we first looked at a night sky, we have transformed the question *why* into the most powerful tool in the history of life.

**WE CALL IT SCIENCE.**

And it is the only human endeavour that gives us a fighting chance against the next twelve-kilometre rock with our name on it.

---

### THE MISSION LOG: 2026

In the last twelve months, human beings have travelled further from Earth than any person alive today had in fifty years, aboard **Artemis 2**. We have landed helicopters on Mars. We have photographed black holes. We have achieved fusion ignition — the moment a star is briefly, controllably born in a laboratory.

We are not a species in decline. We are a species at the beginning.

---

<div class="love-letter">
  <p>"This book is a love letter to the future."</p>
</div>

It is addressed to the child who will be born on Mars. To the engineer who will design the generation ship. To whoever, centuries from now, will find the unmistakable fingerprint of life on a distant world.

**But it is also addressed to you. Right now.**

The question of whether humanity becomes a multi-planetary species is not yet decided. It is being decided now, in laboratories and launch facilities and voting booths.
