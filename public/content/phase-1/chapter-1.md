The *New York Times* is a serious newspaper. It employs serious people. Its editorial pages are read by the educated, the influential, the kind of men who shape the opinions of a nation. And on this morning, in response to the growing enthusiasm for powered human flight — enthusiasm the editors clearly find both charming and delusional — the paper publishes an editorial that will become the single most spectacular act of wrongness in the history of confident journalism.

<div class="newspaper-quote">
  <span class="source">NEW YORK TIMES EDITORIAL</span>
  <span class="date">OCTOBER 9, 1903</span>
  <p>"The flying machine which will really fly might be evolved by the mathematicians and mechanicians of from one to ten million years."</p>
</div>

<p class="impact-text">One to ten million years.</p>

Let that settle in. The *New York Times*, in the autumn of 1903, looked at the available evidence — the failed attempts, the dead pioneers, the sheer audacity of the idea that a heavier-than-air machine could be made to fly through sustained powered flight under human control — and concluded that success was not around the corner, not in a generation, not in a century. It was a million years away. Perhaps ten million. **Geologic time.** The same timescale on which species evolve. The same timescale on which mountain ranges rise and erode.

Sixty-five days later, at 10:35 in the morning on December 17, 1903, at Kill Devil Hills near the town of Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, Orville Wright climbed into the Flyer I — a biplane constructed largely from spruce wood and unbleached muslin — and became the first human being in history to achieve sustained, controlled, powered flight.

The flight lasted twelve seconds. It covered thirty-seven metres — roughly the wingspan of a modern Boeing 737. The altitude reached was perhaps three metres.

The *Times* would not publish a correction.

They wouldn't need to. What happened next would make the correction unnecessary, and redundant, and almost laughably insufficient.

<p class="impact-text cyan">The Times said a million years. The Wright Brothers said: sixty-five days.</p>

### THE STORY OF SIXTY-SIX YEARS

I want to tell you the story of sixty-six years, because it is the most important story in this book. More important than any single chapter that follows. More important, I would argue, than any story in the history of our species — because it is the story that contains all the other stories.

<div class="archive-quote">
  <p>It is the proof, delivered in flight times and rocket trajectories and bootprints on alien soil, that the human imagination consistently, reliably, and spectacularly underestimates itself.</p>
</div>

The twelve seconds at Kitty Hawk were not enough to impress the press. The Dayton Daily News, based in the Wright Brothers' home city, barely covered the story. Most papers didn't bother. The *Times* certainly didn't revisit its editorial. The prevailing wisdom, even after December 17th, was that this flying-machine business was a novelty, a curiosity, a trick that would remain impractical for any serious purpose for a very, very long time.

The Wright Brothers flew again the next day. And the day after.

By 1905, Wilbur Wright was flying circles over Ohio for thirty-nine minutes at a stretch. In 1908, he flew in France before crowds of hundreds of thousands, banked over fields, glided in long lazy arcs that made people weep, and in doing so transformed aviation from a rumour into a reality that the world could no longer ignore.

By **1914** — eleven years after Kitty Hawk — airplanes were being used in combat, dropping bombs, conducting reconnaissance, reshaping the doctrine of war. By **1919**, Alcock and Brown crossed the Atlantic non-stop: 3,040 kilometres of open ocean, in sixteen hours and twenty-seven minutes. By **1927**, Charles Lindbergh crossed it alone, in 33.5 hours, and was received in Paris by a crowd of 150,000 people. By **1933** — thirty years after the *Times* editorial — Boeing was flying commercial passengers across America in enclosed, heated aircraft at 300 kilometres per hour.

Thirty years from twelve seconds over the sand at Kill Devil Hills to the first commercial airlines.

The *Times* had said a million.

But we are nowhere near done.

### PEENEMÜNDE

**1944. The Baltic coast of Germany.**

In a complex of laboratories and launch facilities shrouded in military secrecy, a team of engineers led by a 32-year-old German rocket scientist named Wernher von Braun fires the A-4 rocket — later renamed the V-2 by the Nazi regime that funded it. It is the first man-made object to reach space, crossing the Kármán line at 100 kilometres altitude.

It is also a weapon, and it will kill thousands of civilians across London and Antwerp before the war ends.

<p class="impact-text purple">History is not clean. Progress rarely is.</p>

But the rocket worked. Gravity had been overcome, not with wings and propellers, but with thrust and mathematics and the controlled explosion of liquid oxygen and ethanol. The sky was no longer the limit. The sky, as it turned out, was merely the beginning.

Von Braun was brought to America after the war, along with 1,500 of his colleagues, in a programme called **Operation Paperclip**. His past was complicated and morally compromised in ways that history must not forget. But his engineering was extraordinary, and the Americans who recruited him knew exactly what they were recruiting him for: not defence, not missiles, but the thing that von Braun had dreamed about since he was a teenager reading science fiction in Berlin.

The Moon. The planets. Space.

### THE BURST

What followed was the most concentrated burst of technological progress in human history.

The transistor in **1947**. The first satellite — Sputnik — in **1957**. The first human in space — Yuri Gagarin — in April **1961**, a single orbit of the Earth lasting 108 minutes. Alan Shepard a month later, a fifteen-minute suborbital hop that prompted President Kennedy to stand before Congress and make a promise that was, by any rational measure, insane: that the United States would land a human being on the Moon and return them safely to Earth before the end of the decade.

The decade was nine years away. The United States had **seventeen minutes** of spaceflight experience. The rocket required for the mission did not yet exist. The guidance computers required did not yet exist. The spacesuits, the orbital mechanics, the rendezvous and docking procedures, the lunar module, the navigation systems, the re-entry heat shields — none of it existed.

Kennedy was promising to build, from almost nothing, in less than a decade, the most complex machine ever constructed by human hands.

Forty-eight months after Kitty Hawk, there were commercial airlines.

Forty-eight months after Sputnik, there was Gagarin.

<p class="impact-text cyan">The speed of human audacity, once properly motivated, is not a quantity the cautious mind is equipped to estimate.</p>

### JULY 20, 1969

Let me put you in the room — or rather, on the planet. All of them.

Because on July 20, 1969, for the first and perhaps only time in history, every human being alive on Earth shared the same moment, pointed at the same sky, held the same breath.

There were roughly 3.6 billion people alive. An estimated **600 million** of them were watching on television screens — more people simultaneously experiencing a single event than had ever occurred before in history. The rest knew it was happening. In cities and villages, in living rooms and public squares, in hospital wards and prison cells and classrooms, the world had collectively decided to pay attention.

At 20:17 UTC, the Eagle — the spidery, angular, gold-foil-wrapped lunar module of Apollo 11 — touched the surface of the Moon.

<div class="archive-quote">
  <p><strong>ARMSTRONG (1.3 sec delay):</strong> "Houston, Tranquility Base here. The Eagle has landed."</p>
  <br/>
  <p><strong>DUKE (Mission Control, 26 sec later):</strong> "Roger, Tranquility. We copy you on the ground. You've got a bunch of guys about to turn blue. We're breathing again. Thanks."</p>
</div>

Six hours later, Armstrong descended the nine rungs of the lunar module's ladder, paused at the bottom rung, and stepped onto the surface of another world. His boot sank slightly into fine grey regolith. He said the words you know. And in saying them, he made them true: one small step, one giant leap — a transformation of the human story so sudden, so complete, so unarguable that it remains, fifty-seven years later, the most dramatic single event in the history of our species.

The Moon. That thing in the sky. The thing poets had written about for ten thousand years. The thing sailors navigated by. The thing lovers stared at. The thing shamans prayed to and astronomers mapped and children reached for.

<div class="gut-punch">
We went there.
</div>

We went there sixty-six years after we first flew for twelve seconds.

The *Times* said a million. History answered: sixty-six years.

### THE AUDACITY EQUATION

I am telling you this story not because it is inspiring — though it is — but because it contains a law. A principle as reliable as gravity, as consistent as the speed of light, and far more important for the species that discovered both.

<div class="manifesto-box">
THE TIME REQUIRED FOR HUMANITY TO ACHIEVE A THING DECLARED IMPOSSIBLE IS ALWAYS SHORTER THAN THE DECLARATION SUGGESTS. USUALLY BY SEVERAL ORDERS OF MAGNITUDE. AND THE SPEED INCREASES AS THE TOOLS IMPROVE.
</div>

Think about what changed between 1903 and 1969. The tools — the materials, the mathematics, the computational capacity, the manufacturing precision — improved by a factor that makes comparison almost meaningless.

An Apollo guidance computer had **4,096 words of RAM**. Your phone has more computing power than the entire NASA mission control complex of 1969. The Wright Brothers built their aircraft in a bicycle shop using tools they made themselves. Saturn V was built by **400,000 engineers** across dozens of institutions over eight years.

When the tools improve, the impossible evaporates faster than anyone predicted. This is not optimism. It is data.

### FIFTY YEARS OF SILENCE

Now we must talk about the fifty years that followed.

Because between 1972 and 2025, something uncomfortable happened. **We stopped going.**

Apollo 17, in December 1972, was the last crewed lunar mission. Gene Cernan, the mission commander, was the last human being to stand on the Moon. As he climbed back into the lunar module, he said, quietly:

<div class="archive-quote">
  <p>"We leave as we came, and, God willing, as we shall return — with peace and hope for all mankind."</p>
  <br/>
  <p>— EUGENE CERNAN, 14 DECEMBER 1972</p>
</div>

He was not gone long, he implied. We would be back.

We were not back for fifty years.

The reasons are complicated and not entirely dishonourable — political will, budget constraints, the end of the Space Race's motivating terror, the shift to the Space Shuttle and the ISS and the extraordinary science conducted in low Earth orbit. These were not nothing. The Hubble Space Telescope, deployed in 1990, gave humanity its deepest, clearest view of the universe and changed cosmology permanently. The ISS, occupied continuously since 2000, taught us more about the human body in microgravity than any ground-based study could.

But no human went further than low Earth orbit for fifty years. No human boot touched anything other than this planet and its space stations for half a century.

The frontier, for a while, became administrative.

### THE RETURN

**April 2025.**

The Orion spacecraft, carrying four astronauts — **Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Canadian astronaut Jeremy Hansen** — on the Artemis 2 mission, completes the first crewed flight beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17. They travel to a distance of 10,000 kilometres beyond the far side of the Moon. Further than any human being has travelled from Earth since December 1972.

They come home safely.

<p class="impact-text cyan">The age of deep human exploration has reopened.</p>

And if you know anything about the Audacity Equation, you know what comes next. You know that once the capability is re-demonstrated, once the engineers remember how to do it and the politicians remember why it matters and the public remembers how it feels — you know that things accelerate. Fast. Faster than anyone in the boardrooms and editorial offices is currently predicting.

NASA's Artemis programme intends to establish a sustained human presence on the lunar surface. Not visits. Not flags-and-footprints. **A base.** A permanent outpost. The SR-1 Freedom nuclear fission reactor is designed specifically to power it — because solar panels alone cannot sustain human habitation through the fourteen-day lunar night. SpaceX's Starship, the largest and most powerful rocket ever flown, has completed multiple orbital test flights and is manifested for crewed missions. Blue Origin, Rocket Lab, United Launch Alliance — the launch cadence is accelerating in ways the industry has never seen.

---

### MARS IS WAITING

Rovers are on it right now, this second, analysing rocks and drilling cores and searching for biosignatures of ancient microbial life in the oxidised red dust.

**Ingenuity** — a helicopter the size of a tissue box, with two kilograms of mass and nothing but two rotors, four legs, and a solar panel — made the first powered, controlled flight on another planet on April 19, 2021.

<p class="impact-text purple">The Wright Brothers of another world.</p>

The first 39-second flight on Mars. The first of many.

No human has been there yet.

<div class="gut-punch">
That sentence has an expiration date.
</div>

### A QUESTION ABOUT GOD

I want to pause here and do something that will make some readers uncomfortable, because I think discomfort is sometimes the most honest form of respect.

I want to talk about what the Wright Brothers, and Gagarin, and Armstrong, and the engineers of Artemis 2, have to do with God.

Not in the way you might expect. Not to dismiss faith, or to wave science triumphantly in the face of religion, or to score a point. But to ask a genuine question: what does the Audacity Equation tell us about the nature of human progress and the frameworks we use to navigate it?

Here is what I know.

Orville Wright was a man who attended church. Neil Armstrong was a man who, in his own quiet way, believed in something larger than himself. Many of the engineers who built Apollo were people of sincere religious faith. **I am not arguing that faith and science are incompatible in the individual human heart. History disproves that argument on every page.**

What I am arguing is this:

Not one gram of lift at Kitty Hawk came from prayer. Not one metre of altitude over the Sea of Tranquility was achieved by faith. The Moon landing was the product of **mathematics** — specifically the mathematics of Newtonian mechanics and Keplerian orbital trajectories. Of **chemistry** — specifically the chemistry of liquid oxygen and liquid hydrogen and the specific impulse of the J-2 engine. Of materials science and metallurgy and computing and navigation and a thousand disciplines built painstakingly over centuries, each one passing its knowledge to the next, each one standing on the shoulders of the ones before.

The Wright Brothers succeeded where Samuel Langley, Hiram Maxim, and dozens of other brilliant, serious, well-funded men failed, not because God chose them, but because they understood something the others missed: **that control, not just lift, was the fundamental problem.** Their insight was aeronautical, not theological.

Armstrong stepped on the Moon because on May 25, 1961, John Kennedy made a political decision to fund a technological programme, and 400,000 engineers spent eight years solving one specific problem after another with physics. The moment any one of those problems — re-entry heating, oxygen supply, guidance computers, rocket staging — had been answered with "God will provide," Armstrong would have died, and we would not remember his name.

I do not say this harshly. I say it with enormous respect for what those engineers accomplished. Their success was a monument to human reasoning, human persistence, human ingenuity. It was perhaps the finest expression of what our species can do when it decides to use its mind.

And the mind is — let me be clear about this, because I believe it with every professional conviction I have — the most sacred thing in the known universe. Because it is, as far as we can determine, the only thing in the known universe that can ask the question *why*.

<p class="impact-text purple">The mind is the most sacred thing in the universe.</p>

That mind carried us to the Moon.

That mind, and only that mind, will carry us to the stars.
